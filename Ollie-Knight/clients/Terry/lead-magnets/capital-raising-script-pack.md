# SCRIPT 1: THE WARM APPROACH

## For Friends, Family, and People Already in Your Phone Book

This is where most of my students start, and honestly, where most of the money comes from. You already have trust with these people. You just need to channel it properly.

### Step 1: The Setup Text

Don't call someone out of the blue and pitch them. That's weird. You need to plant the seed first. Send this text:

---

> **The Text:**
>
> "Hey [Name], hope you're well! Quick one, I've been looking into something quite exciting on the property side, specifically short-let Airbnb properties. I've been learning from someone who built a portfolio of 14 units as a law student at Warwick with none of his own money and I'm putting my first deal together. Not asking for anything right now, but I thought of you because I know you're smart with your money. Would you be up for a quick coffee/call this week? I'd love to get your thoughts on it. No pressure at all."

---

**Why this works:**

- You're not asking for money (yet). You're asking for their "thoughts." People love giving advice.
- You're positioning yourself as someone who's learning, not someone who's desperate.
- The Terry reference gives instant credibility, a real person, real results, real system.
- "No pressure at all" removes resistance before it even builds.

### Step 2: The Meeting Script

Whether it's a coffee, a phone call, or a Zoom, here's the flow. Don't memorise it robotically. Understand the structure and make it yours.

---

**Opening (2 minutes):**

"Thanks for taking the time, I really appreciate it. So basically, I've been studying the short-let property space, Airbnb, Booking.com, that side of things. I've been going through a programme with a guy called Terry Dwobeng, he built 14 Airbnb units while he was at uni, no money of his own, was making around £50k/month in revenue and £16k/month in profit at his peak. And he's taught over 25 students who've done similar things. I've been following his system and I'm now putting together my first unit."

**The Opportunity (3 minutes):**

"Here's what I've found, there are properties out there where you can take a lease or a management agreement, furnish them, list them on Airbnb, and generate really strong cash flow. I'm talking net profits of £1,000-£2,000 per unit per month once it's up and running. The setup cost for a unit is usually somewhere between £3,000-£8,000 depending on the property, that's furniture, photos, listing setup, the lot. And you can be cash-flow positive within the first 30-60 days."

**The Ask (2 minutes):**

"So here's where I wanted to get your thoughts. To get my first unit up, I need around £[amount] for setup costs. I'm looking at working with a small number of people who want to put their money to work and get a proper return on it, way better than what a savings account gives you. The structure would be [choose one: a profit share / a fixed return of X% / their money back within X months plus a share of profits]. I'm not asking you to decide anything now. I just wanted to share it with you first because I trust you and I thought you might find it interesting."

**Close:**

"Have a think about it. No rush. I can send you a one-page breakdown of the numbers if that would help? And if it's not for you, absolutely no hard feelings, I'd still appreciate any advice you've got."

---

### Step 3: Handling "Let Me Think About It"

This is the most common response and it's actually a GOOD sign. It means they're interested but not ready. Here's what you do:

**In the moment, say:**

> "Of course, take your time. Genuinely no pressure. Would it help if I sent you a one-page summary you can look at in your own time? And shall I check back in with you in a few days?"

**Then follow up 3-4 days later:**

> "Hey [Name], just following up from our chat. Did you get a chance to look at that summary I sent? Happy to answer any questions. Also just to let you know, I'm looking to get started within the next [timeframe] so if it's something you're interested in, it would be great to have a chat this week. But again, no pressure, I value our relationship way more than any deal."

**The key:** Always give them an easy out. Paradoxically, the less pressure you put on, the more likely they are to say yes. Nobody wants to feel sold to. They want to feel like they're making a smart decision.

# OBJECTION HANDLING

## The 5 Most Common Investor Objections (and Exactly What to Say)

You're going to hear objections. Every single time. That's normal. An objection isn't a "no", it's a "tell me more." Here's how to handle the big five.

---

### Objection 1: "Sounds risky"

**What to say:**

"I hear you, and I'd be worried if you DIDN'T say that, it means you're thinking about it properly. Here's the thing though, everything has risk. Leaving your money in a savings account has risk, inflation is eating it alive. Property has been one of the most reliable asset classes in the UK for decades. And with short-lets specifically, we're not buying a property, so there's no mortgage risk. The setup cost is relatively small, the income starts within weeks, and I've got a mentor who's done this 14 times over guiding me through every step. It's not zero risk, nothing is. But it's calculated, it's structured, and honestly, the bigger risk might be leaving your money where it is doing nothing."

---

### Objection 2: "I don't know anything about Airbnb"

**What to say:**

"That's actually perfect, because you don't need to. You're not running the Airbnb. I am. Your role is the investor. You put in the capital, I do all the work, finding the property, furnishing it, managing the guests, handling the day-to-day. You just receive your return. Think of it like investing in a restaurant, you don't need to know how to cook. You just need to trust the chef. And in this case, I'm following a recipe from someone who's already done it 14 times."

---

### Objection 3: "What if it doesn't work?"

**What to say:**

"Good question. Let's talk about what 'not working' actually looks like. Worst case scenario, the property doesn't perform as well as projected. Even at 50% of our conservative estimates, we'd still be covering costs and generating some return. But the system I'm following has been used by over 25 students successfully. My mentor Terry has done it 14 times. I'm not inventing something new, I'm following a proven playbook. And I'm starting with one unit specifically so we can prove the model before scaling. If for whatever reason we needed to wind it down, the exit is straightforward, we end the lease, sell the furniture, and return your capital. It's not like buying a house where you're locked in."

---

### Objection 4: "How do I get my money back?"

**What to say:**

"Great question, and this is something I've thought about carefully. Here's the structure: your initial investment of £[amount] will be returned to you from the profits within [X months]. Once your capital is returned, we move into the ongoing profit share. If at any point you want to exit completely, we agree a notice period, say 30 or 60 days, wind down that unit, sell the furniture, and you're out. I'll put all of this in a simple written agreement before you put in a penny so everything is clear from day one. Your money isn't locked away. This isn't a 5-year fixed term. It's flexible and transparent."

---

### Objection 5: "Why should I trust you with my money?"

**What to say:**

"Honestly? That's the most important question you could ask, and I respect you for asking it. Here's why: I'm not doing this alone. I'm following a system from Terry Dwobeng at Property Labs, he built 14 Airbnb units while studying law at Warwick, zero of his own money, £50k/month in revenue and £16k/month in profit at peak. His students have collectively raised over £200k and set up units across the UK. I'm trained, I'm mentored, and I'm accountable. I'll give you full transparency, monthly reports, access to booking data, everything. And I'm putting this in a written agreement so it's all documented. But beyond all that, you know me. You know my character. I'm putting my reputation on the line here, and that matters more to me than anything. I'm not going to mess that up."

---

**Pro tip:** After handling any objection, always ask: "Does that make sense? What other questions do you have?" Don't just answer and go silent. Keep the conversation moving. The person who asks the questions controls the conversation.
