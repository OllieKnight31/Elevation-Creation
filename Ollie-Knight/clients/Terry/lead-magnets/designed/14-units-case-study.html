The Beginning

The Starting Point

No savings. No property experience. No family money. Just a phone, an internet connection, and a gut feeling.

Let me take you back to where this all began.

I was a uni student. No savings. No property experience. No family money sitting in the background. No rich uncle. No trust fund. Nothing.

I had exactly what most people reading this have right now — a phone, an internet connection, and a feeling in my gut that the traditional path wasn't going to cut it.

Everyone around me was saying the same thing. "Get your degree, Terry. Get a good job. Save up for a few years. Maybe buy a house when you're 30."

Graduate at 21. Start on maybe £25k a year. Save £300–£400 a month. That's five, six, seven years before a deposit on one property. And by then, prices have gone up again. That wasn't a plan. That was a treadmill.

So I refused to accept it. Not because I thought I was special. But because I genuinely believed there had to be another way — a way to build something real, right now, without waiting for permission or a payslip to catch up with the market.

This case study is the full breakdown of what happened next. Month by month. The real numbers. The real mistakes. And the real results.

The Journey

Month-by-Month Timeline

From zero to 14 profitable Airbnb units — every phase, laid bare.

Months 1–2
Building Credibility (Before I Spent a Penny)

Most people want to skip this bit. They want to jump straight to "how do I get a property?" But this phase is the reason everything else worked.

Educated myself obsessively. Courses, YouTube, podcasts, books — anything on serviced accommodation, Airbnb, and short-term lets in the UK. Every spare hour went into understanding the model inside out.

Learned the numbers. Average nightly rates, occupancy rates by season, cleaning turnaround costs, margins — that knowledge became my currency.

Networked relentlessly. Property meetups, Facebook groups, LinkedIn. I put myself in rooms with people already doing what I wanted to do.

Built a presence. Started posting on social media about what I was learning. Not flexing. Just sharing my journey honestly. That consistency made people see me as someone who knew what he was talking about.

By the end of month two, I still didn't have a property or any money. But I had something more valuable — credibility.

Months 3–4
First Capital Raised

I'd built up enough knowledge and credibility that when I presented an opportunity to potential investors, they could see I'd done the work. I wasn't some random kid asking for a handout.

My first investor wasn't a millionaire. They were a regular person with savings sitting in a bank account earning basically nothing. I showed them what those savings could do in serviced accommodation.

No banks. No mortgages in my name. No personal savings deployed. Just a solid proposal backed by real knowledge and genuine credibility.

Months 5–6
First Airbnb Secured

I secured a 2-bedroom apartment through a rent-to-serviced-accommodation arrangement (rent-to-SA). The landlord got guaranteed rent, paid on time every month. I got the spread between rent and nightly bookings.

Months 7–12
Scaling to Multiple Units

Once I had proof of concept — a live, profitable unit with real guest reviews and real income — everything accelerated. Raising capital became easier. Landlords were easier to approach.

Each new unit followed the same formula:

  1. Find the right property — location, layout, and numbers had to work
  2. Secure the landlord with a professional guaranteed-rent proposal
  3. Raise setup capital with a proven track record
  4. Furnish and list — guest-ready and live within 2–3 weeks
  5. Systemise — cleaning teams, automated messaging, dynamic pricing, SOPs

The key shift: moving from doing everything myself to building systems. The business started to run like a business, not a side hustle.

Year 2+
14 Units and £16k/Month

By the time I hit 14 units, the portfolio was generating £16,000 per month at its peak. A team, systems, and processes let me focus on growth and strategy rather than being stuck in the weeds.

All of this — every single unit — built without using a single penny of my own money.

Lessons Learned

The 3 Biggest Mistakes I Made

I'm not here to paint a perfect picture. Some of these were expensive.

01
Trying to Do Everything Alone
Guest comms, cleaning coordination, listing optimisation, investor relations, landlord management — I was doing ALL of it.
What It Cost Me
Burnout, missed messages, slower response times that affected reviews, and nearly put me off the whole thing.
The Fix
Bring in help from unit one. Even a VA handling guest messages for a few hours a day makes a massive difference.
02
Choosing the Wrong Property Type
One early unit looked great on paper but was a nightmare operationally. Wrong layout, awkward access, wrong type of demand.
What It Cost Me
Lower occupancy, more complaints, higher maintenance, and an early exit — wasted time and setup capital.
The Fix
Be ruthless about property selection. Right layout, right location (demand drivers nearby), right landlord.
03
Not Having Systems From Day One
No proper CRM, no automated messaging, no standardised checklists, no SOPs. Just me, reacting to things as they came up.
What It Cost Me
Inconsistent guest experiences, missed details during turnovers, tanked reviews from small slip-ups compounding.
The Fix
Set up systems before the first unit goes live. Automated messaging, cleaning checklists, pricing strategy, maintenance protocols.

If I Started Today

What I'd Do Differently

If I was starting from absolute zero in 2026, here's exactly what I'd do — in this order.

  1. Learn the model inside out
    Give yourself 2–4 weeks of intensive education. Understand serviced accommodation, rent-to-SA, legal requirements, the financial model. Don't wing it.
  2. Pick ONE city and become the expert
    Choose a city with strong demand drivers — universities, hospitals, business parks, transport links — and learn everything about it.
  3. Build your credibility online
    Start posting. Share what you're learning. Do deal analyses publicly. This is how you attract investors and landlords without cold-calling.
  4. Network with purpose
    Property events, online communities, local meetups. Build genuine relationships. Don't pitch anyone in the first conversation.
  5. Create your investor proposal
    Professional, clear, compelling. The numbers, returns, risk mitigation, exit strategy. Make it impossible for a reasonable person to say no.
  6. Secure your first investor
    Start with people in your network who have savings earning nothing. Present the opportunity. Be honest, transparent, professional.
  7. Find and secure your first property
    Use your market knowledge to identify the right property. Approach the landlord with a professional proposal. Negotiate terms that work for both sides.
  8. Get it live and profitable within 3 weeks
    Furnish it, photograph it, list it, start taking bookings. Don't overthink. Get it live, get reviews, optimise from there.
  9. Build your systems immediately
    Don't wait until you're drowning. Automated messaging, cleaning processes, pricing tools, SOPs — all from day one.
  10. Repeat
    Use the results from unit one to raise more capital and secure more properties. Each one gets easier than the last.
