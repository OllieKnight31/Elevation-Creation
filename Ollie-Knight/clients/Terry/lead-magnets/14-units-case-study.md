## The Month-by-Month Timeline

### Months 1–2: Building Credibility (Before I Spent a Penny)

Most people want to skip this bit. They want to jump straight to "how do I get a property?" But this phase is the reason everything else worked.

Here's what I actually did in those first two months:

**I educated myself obsessively.** I'm talking courses, YouTube, podcasts, books, anything on serviced accommodation, Airbnb, and short-term lets in the UK. I wasn't casually watching a video here and there. I was treating it like a second degree. Every spare hour went into understanding the model inside out.

**I learned the numbers.** Before I ever spoke to an investor or a landlord, I could tell you the average nightly rate for a 2-bed in Manchester, Birmingham, Leeds, Nottingham. I knew what occupancy rates looked like by season. I knew what a cleaning turnaround cost. I knew what the margins were. That knowledge became my currency.

**I networked relentlessly.** Property meetups. Facebook groups. LinkedIn. I put myself in rooms with people who were already doing what I wanted to do. Not to pitch them. Not to ask for money. Just to learn, to listen, and to let them see that I was serious.

**I built a presence.** I started posting on social media about what I was learning. Not flexing. Not pretending I had results I didn't have. Just sharing my journey honestly. "Here's what I learned this week about serviced accommodation." "Here's a deal analysis I did on a 2-bed in Sheffield." That consistency did something powerful. It made people start to see me as someone who knew what he was talking about.

By the end of month two, I still didn't have a property. I still didn't have any money. But I had something more valuable. Credibility. People knew who I was. They knew I was serious. And they were starting to pay attention.

### Months 3–4: First Capital Raised

This is where most people's jaws drop. Because the question I always get is: "But Terry, if you had no money, how did you start?"

The answer is simple. I used other people's money. And before you think that sounds dodgy or unrealistic, let me explain exactly how.

By this point, I'd built up enough knowledge and credibility that when I presented an opportunity to potential investors, they could see I'd done the work. I wasn't some random kid asking for a handout. I was someone who could walk them through the numbers, show them the market research, explain the model, and demonstrate exactly how their money would be used and returned.

My first investor wasn't a millionaire. They were a regular person with some savings sitting in a bank account earning basically nothing. I showed them what those savings could do in serviced accommodation, the projected returns, the security, the exit plan. And they said yes.

I raised my first chunk of capital, enough to cover a deposit, furnishing, and setup costs for my first unit. The deal was structured so the investor got a return that beat anything their bank was offering, and I got to operate the property.

No banks. No mortgages in my name. No personal savings deployed. Just a solid proposal backed by real knowledge and genuine credibility.

### Months 5–6: First Airbnb Secured

Getting that first unit live is a feeling I'll never forget.

I secured a 2-bedroom apartment through a rent-to-serviced-accommodation arrangement, commonly known as rent-to-SA. I wasn't buying the property. I was renting it from a landlord at a fixed monthly rate and then subletting it as a short-term let on Airbnb and Booking.com.

The landlord got guaranteed rent, paid on time every month, with the property maintained to a high standard. I got the spread between what I paid in rent and what I earned from nightly bookings.

The property was in a strong UK city centre location, the kind of area with consistent demand from business travellers, contractors, tourists, and families visiting nearby hospitals or universities. Good transport links. Restaurants and shops within walking distance.

**The numbers on that first unit:**

- Monthly rent to landlord: £850
- Average nightly rate on Airbnb: £95
- Average occupancy: 78%
- Monthly revenue: ~£2,200
- Monthly costs (rent, bills, cleaning, consumables, platform fees): ~£1,400
- **Monthly profit: ~£800**

£800 a month from one unit. While I was still at uni.

That first unit taught me everything. How to manage guest communications. How to handle turnovers. How to deal with the odd complaint. How to optimise a listing. How to price dynamically. It was my training ground.

### Months 7–12: Scaling to Multiple Units

Once I had proof of concept, a live, profitable unit with real guest reviews and real income, everything accelerated.

Raising capital became easier because I could show actual results, not just projections. Landlords were easier to approach because I could point to my existing unit and say, "Look, this is what I do. Here are my reviews. Here's how I maintain the property. Here's the guaranteed rent I pay."

Between months seven and twelve, I went from one unit to multiple units across different areas. Each new unit followed the same formula:

1. **Find the right property.** Location, layout, and numbers had to work.
2. **Secure the landlord.** Present a professional proposal with guaranteed rent.
3. **Raise the setup capital.** Approach investors with a proven track record.
4. **Furnish and list.** Get the property guest-ready and live on platforms within 2–3 weeks.
5. **Systemise.** Cleaning teams, automated messaging, dynamic pricing, key handover processes.

The key shift during this period was moving from doing everything myself to building systems. I couldn't be responding to every guest message, doing every check-in, coordinating every clean. I had to build a machine that ran without me being hands-on 24/7.

I brought on a cleaning team. I set up automated messages for guests. I created checklists and SOPs (standard operating procedures) for every single task. The business started to run like a business, not a side hustle.

### Year 2+: Reaching 14 Units and £16k/Month in Profit

By the time I hit 14 units, the portfolio was generating £50,000 per month in revenue and £16,000 per month in profit at its peak.

Let me be clear, that fluctuated. Some months were higher, some lower. Seasonality matters. January is different from August. But the systems, the processes, and the portfolio diversification meant that even in quieter months, the numbers were strong.

At this point, I wasn't managing day-to-day operations myself anymore. I had a team. I had systems. I had processes that let me focus on growth and strategy rather than being stuck in the weeds.

All of this, every single unit, built without using a single penny of my own money.

## The 3 Biggest Mistakes I Made

I'm not here to paint a perfect picture. I made mistakes. Some of them were expensive. Here's what I'd warn you about.

### Mistake 1: Trying to Do Everything Alone

In the early days, I thought I had to be a one-man army. Guest comms, cleaning coordination, listing optimisation, investor relations, landlord management, I was doing ALL of it.

**What it cost me:** Burnout, missed messages, slower response times to guests which affected my reviews, and honestly it nearly put me off the whole thing. There were nights where I was dealing with a guest issue at 11pm, knowing I had a lecture at 9am, and thinking "is this actually worth it?"

**What I'd do differently:** I'd bring in help from unit one. Even if it's just a virtual assistant handling guest messages for a few hours a day. You don't need a full team from the start, but you need to accept that you can't scale while doing every single task yourself.

### Mistake 2: Choosing the Wrong Property Type Early On

One of my early units was a property that looked great on paper but was a nightmare operationally. Wrong layout for Airbnb guests. Awkward access. In an area that had demand but not the RIGHT kind of demand for short stays.

**What it cost me:** Lower occupancy than projected, more guest complaints, higher maintenance costs, and ultimately lower margins. I ended up exiting that property after several months, which meant I'd wasted time and setup capital.

**What I'd do differently:** I'd be ruthless about property selection. Not every property works for serviced accommodation. You need the right layout (open plan living, comfortable sleeping arrangements, good bathroom facilities), the right location (demand drivers nearby), and the right landlord (someone who actually understands and supports the model).

### Mistake 3: Not Having Systems From Day One

For the first few units, I was running everything from my phone. No proper CRM. No automated messaging. No standardised cleaning checklists. No SOPs. Just me, reacting to things as they came up.

**What it cost me:** Inconsistent guest experiences, missed details during turnovers, and a business that felt chaotic rather than controlled. When you're running multiple units without systems, things slip through the cracks. A forgotten toiletry restock here, a late check-in instruction there, these small things add up and tank your reviews.

**What I'd do differently:** I'd set up my systems before I even got my first unit live. Automated guest messaging templates. A cleaning checklist that covers every single item. A pricing strategy. A maintenance response protocol. Build the infrastructure first, then plug properties into it.

## What I'd Do Differently If I Started Today

If I was starting from absolute zero in 2026, here's exactly what I'd do, in this order:

**Step 1: Learn the model inside out.** Give yourself 2–4 weeks of intensive education. Understand serviced accommodation, the rent-to-SA model, the legal requirements, the financial model. Don't wing it.

**Step 2: Pick ONE city and become the expert.** Don't try to understand every market in the UK. Pick one city, ideally one with strong demand drivers (universities, hospitals, business parks, transport links, tourist attractions), and learn everything about it. Average rents, average nightly rates, occupancy trends, local regulations.

**Step 3: Build your credibility online.** Start posting. Share what you're learning. Do deal analyses publicly. Show people you're serious. This is not optional. This is how you attract investors and landlords without cold-calling hundreds of people.

**Step 4: Network with purpose.** Get into property networking events, online communities, and local meetups. Build genuine relationships. Don't pitch anyone in the first conversation. Just be present, be helpful, and be consistent.

**Step 5: Create your investor proposal.** Before you approach a single investor, have a professional, clear, compelling proposal ready. The numbers. The returns. The risk mitigation. The exit strategy. Make it impossible for a reasonable person to say no.

**Step 6: Secure your first investor.** Start with people in your network who have savings earning nothing in the bank. Present the opportunity. Be honest, be transparent, be professional.

**Step 7: Find and secure your first property.** Use your market knowledge to identify the right property. Approach the landlord with a professional proposal. Negotiate terms that work for both sides.

**Step 8: Get it live and profitable within 3 weeks.** Furnish it, photograph it, list it, and start taking bookings. Don't overthink this. Get it live, get reviews, optimise from there.

**Step 9: Build your systems immediately.** Don't wait until you're drowning. Set up your automated messaging, cleaning processes, pricing tools, and SOPs from day one.

**Step 10: Repeat.** Use the results from unit one to raise more capital and secure more properties. Each one gets easier than the last.

If I followed these steps today, with what I know now, I genuinely believe I could get to 3–5 profitable units within 6 months. The model hasn't changed. The opportunity is still massive. The only question is whether you're willing to do the work.
