## Introduction: Why Location Is Everything

Here's the truth most Airbnb "gurus" won't tell you: **location is the single biggest factor that determines whether your short-term let makes money or drains your bank account.**

I know because I've built a portfolio of 14 units across the UK. Some of them print money. Others, early on, before I learned this lesson, were a grind.

The mistake I see over and over again? People default to the "obvious" cities. London. Central Manchester. Edinburgh. They think: big city, lots of tourists, easy money.

Wrong.

Those locations are saturated. The competition is brutal. The regulation is tightening. And the numbers, once you factor in rent, bills, furnishing, and management, rarely stack up the way people think they will.

The operators who are quietly making serious, consistent income? They're in places most people overlook. Locations where tourist demand is strong, business travel is steady, but the Airbnb supply hasn't caught up yet.

That gap between demand and supply is where the money is.

I'm going to show you five of those locations right now. These are areas I've either operated in myself or have analysed closely with operators in my network. The numbers are realistic. The opportunity is real. But it won't last forever, these markets are growing.

Let's get into it.

## Hotspot 1: Bath, Somerset

Bath flies under the radar for a lot of new operators, but the numbers here are genuinely strong, and they're consistent year-round, which is rare outside London.

- **Average nightly rate:** £135
- **Average occupancy:** 74%
- **Monthly revenue potential:** £2,800–£3,000
- **Why it works:** Bath is a UNESCO World Heritage Site. It pulls in international tourists, domestic weekend visitors, and a surprising amount of corporate travel thanks to the professional services firms based there and in nearby Bristol. The Roman Baths, the Royal Crescent, the Christmas markets, there's always something drawing people in. Plus, the University of Bath brings parent visits, graduation stays, and open day traffic.
- **Best property type:** 1-bed or 2-bed period apartment in the city centre or within a 10-minute walk. Georgian-style interiors photograph beautifully and command premium rates.
- **Watch out for:** Bath & North East Somerset Council has specific planning requirements for short-term lets. Check whether the property requires a change of use, particularly if it's in a conservation area. Don't skip this step.

## Hotspot 2: Liverpool Waterfront & Baltic Triangle

Liverpool has been on a tear over the last few years. The waterfront and Baltic Triangle areas specifically have seen massive regeneration, and the Airbnb demand has grown with it, but supply is still catching up.

- **Average nightly rate:** £105
- **Average occupancy:** 72%
- **Monthly revenue potential:** £2,100–£2,400
- **Why it works:** Liverpool pulls in stag and hen parties, football tourism (Anfield and Goodison Park weekends are gold), conference delegates at the ACC Liverpool, and a strong music and culture scene. The cost base is significantly lower than London or Manchester, so your margins are better even at lower nightly rates. The city also has a growing digital and creative sector bringing in midweek business travellers.
- **Best property type:** Modern 2-bed apartment in the Baltic Triangle or waterfront area. Stylish interiors do well here, this is a younger demographic who care about aesthetics and Instagram-worthy stays.
- **Watch out for:** Some new-build apartment blocks have restrictive leases that explicitly prohibit short-term letting. Read the lease carefully before committing. If you're doing rent-to-SA, get the landlord's written permission and check the building management company's rules.

## Hotspot 3: Stratford-upon-Avon, Warwickshire

This one surprises people, but Stratford-upon-Avon is a cash machine for Airbnb if you set it up right. It's a small town with enormous, year-round tourist demand.

- **Average nightly rate:** £120
- **Average occupancy:** 70%
- **Monthly revenue potential:** £2,300–£2,600
- **Why it works:** Shakespeare's birthplace. The Royal Shakespeare Company runs performances almost every day of the year, and every single audience member needs somewhere to stay. Add in international coach tours, school trips, and the Cotswolds day-trip crowd using Stratford as a base, and you've got demand that doesn't dry up in winter the way coastal locations do. It's also well-positioned for the NEC and Birmingham, so you catch overspill from major events and conferences.
- **Best property type:** Characterful 2-bed cottage or apartment within walking distance of the town centre and RSC theatre. Period features and a "quintessentially English" feel command a premium from international guests.
- **Watch out for:** Parking. The town centre has limited parking, and guests, especially international visitors with hire cars, will leave negative reviews if they can't park easily. Prioritise properties with a dedicated parking space or very clear parking instructions.

## Hotspot 4: Cardiff Bay, South Wales

Cardiff Bay has transformed over the last decade, and it's now one of the strongest short-term let markets in the UK that most English operators completely ignore.

- **Average nightly rate:** £95
- **Average occupancy:** 71%
- **Monthly revenue potential:** £1,800–£2,100
- **Why it works:** The Principality Stadium alone generates massive demand, Six Nations weekends, concerts, and major events can push nightly rates to £200+. But even outside event weekends, Cardiff Bay has steady demand from corporate travellers, BBC and media production crews (BBC Wales and Bad Wolf Studios are based here), and leisure visitors. The Welsh Government offices bring consistent midweek bookings. And the cost of entry is significantly lower than English cities, you can furnish a 2-bed flat to a high standard for under £5,000.
- **Best property type:** 2-bed apartment in Cardiff Bay or the city centre, ideally within walking distance of the Principality Stadium and the Bay waterfront. Modern, clean, well-equipped units outperform here.
- **Watch out for:** The Welsh Government introduced a licensing scheme for short-term lets in 2025. Make sure you're registered and compliant, it's straightforward, but you need to do it. Non-compliance carries fines.

## Hotspot 5: York (Outside the City Walls)

Everyone knows York is a tourist city. But most operators make the mistake of trying to compete inside the city walls, where prices are high and competition is fierce. The smart move? Look just outside.

- **Average nightly rate:** £110
- **Average occupancy:** 73%
- **Monthly revenue potential:** £2,200–£2,500
- **Why it works:** York attracts over 8 million visitors a year. The Minster, the Shambles, the Christmas markets, the racecourse, demand is enormous and spread across the year. Properties just outside the city walls (Bishopthorpe Road, Holgate, South Bank) are a 10-15 minute walk to the centre but cost 30-40% less to rent or buy. You get the same guest demand at a fraction of the cost base, which means your margins are significantly better. The University of York also drives bookings during graduation, open days, and term-time parent visits.
- **Best property type:** 2-bed terraced house or ground-floor flat with outdoor space. Families and couples visiting York love having a bit more room than a hotel, and a small garden or courtyard is a genuine differentiator.
- **Watch out for:** City of York Council has been consulting on short-term let regulations. Keep an eye on any planning policy changes, the political mood in York is shifting towards tighter controls, so stay informed and make sure you're operating within the rules.

## 3 Locations to AVOID (And Why)

Not every popular city is a good Airbnb market. Here are three "obvious" locations where new operators consistently lose money or burn out.

### Avoid 1: London Zone 1-2

The nightly rates look incredible on paper, £180, £200, £250 a night. But here's what nobody mentions:

- **Rent is astronomical.** A 1-bed in Zone 1-2 can cost £2,000-£2,800/month before you've spent a penny on furnishing, bills, or linen.
- **The 90-night rule.** In London, you can only let a property short-term for 90 nights per calendar year without planning permission. After that, you need to switch to longer lets or leave it empty. This absolutely kills your revenue potential.
- **Competition is savage.** There are over 80,000 Airbnb listings in London. You're competing with professional management companies, hotels offering Airbnb-style stays, and thousands of other operators, all fighting for the same guests.
- **The numbers rarely work for beginners.** By the time you factor in the 90-night cap, the high rent, cleaning costs, and the furnishing spend, most Zone 1-2 units barely break even, if they break even at all.

**My advice:** Unless you own the property outright and have no mortgage, London Zone 1-2 is not where you start.

### Avoid 2: Central Manchester

Manchester was a goldmine for Airbnb operators five years ago. It's not anymore.

- **Oversaturated.** The city centre has seen a huge wave of new-build apartments, and a significant percentage are being used for short-term lets. Supply has outstripped demand.
- **Regulation is tightening.** Manchester City Council has been vocal about cracking down on short-term lets in residential buildings, and several major developments have introduced or enforced lease clauses banning Airbnb-style use.
- **Race to the bottom on price.** With so many listings, operators are undercutting each other. Average nightly rates in central Manchester have dropped, but rents haven't. The margin has been squeezed out.
- **Guest expectations are high.** Because there's so much choice, guests expect hotel-level quality. One bad review and your listing drops off a cliff.

**My advice:** If you want to operate in Greater Manchester, look at Salford Quays, Didsbury, or even Stockport, areas with lower costs and less competition.

### Avoid 3: Edinburgh Old Town

Edinburgh is a world-class tourist destination. It's also one of the most regulated short-term let markets in the UK.

- **Licensing scheme.** Edinburgh introduced a mandatory short-term let licensing scheme, and the council has been actively refusing licences in certain residential areas. Getting a licence in the Old Town is not guaranteed.
- **Seasonal dependency.** Yes, Edinburgh Festival in August is phenomenal, you can charge £300+ a night. But January through March? Occupancy drops significantly. The annual average is much less impressive than the peak suggests.
- **High entry costs.** Property prices and rents in the Old Town are premium. Combined with the licensing fees, insurance requirements, and safety compliance costs, your breakeven point is high.
- **Political risk.** The Scottish Government has given councils significant powers to create "control areas" where short-term lets can be restricted or banned entirely. Edinburgh has already designated the entire city as a control area.

**My advice:** Edinburgh can work, but not for beginners, and not in the Old Town. If you're experienced and understand the licensing process, look at Leith or Portobello for better margins and less regulatory friction.

## How to Validate ANY Location Yourself

You don't have to take my word for it. Before you commit to any area, run it through this 5-step validation process. This is exactly what I do before I take on a new unit.

### Step 1: Check Airbnb for Similar Listings

Go to Airbnb and search for properties similar to what you'd offer (same size, same type) in your target area. Look at:

- **How many listings are there?** If there are hundreds, competition is high. If there are 20-50, that's a sweet spot.
- **What are they charging per night?** Note the average, not the outliers.
- **What do the reviews say?** Read the 3-star reviews, they tell you what guests actually care about in that area.
- **How far in advance are they booked?** If calendars are blocked out 2-3 months ahead, demand is strong. If they're wide open, that's a red flag.

### Step 2: Use AirDNA or Similar for Occupancy Data

AirDNA (airdna.co) gives you actual market data, average occupancy rates, revenue estimates, and seasonal trends for any area. The free version gives you enough to make a decision. Look for:

- **Annual occupancy above 65%.** Below that, your revenue will be inconsistent.
- **Revenue trends.** Is the market growing or declining? You want to enter markets that are trending upward.
- **Seasonal patterns.** How dramatic is the difference between peak and off-peak? Ideally, you want a market where off-peak is still decent.

### Step 3: Check Local Council Short-Term Let Regulations

This is the step most people skip, and it's the one that can destroy your business overnight.

- Google "[council name] short-term let regulations" or "short-term let planning permission."
- Check whether the council requires a licence, registration, or planning permission for short-term lets.
- Look for any recent council meeting minutes or planning documents that mention short-term lets, this tells you which way the political wind is blowing.
- If in doubt, call the council's planning department directly and ask. They'll tell you.

### Step 4: Call 3 Local Letting Agents and Ask About Demand

This is old-school, but it works. Call three letting agents in the area and ask:

- "What's the demand like for short-term furnished lets in this area?"
- "What would a [2-bed flat] rent for on a standard AST versus a short-term basis?"
- "Are you seeing more landlords moving into serviced accommodation?"

Their answers will tell you whether the market is heating up, cooling down, or saturated. If they all say "everyone's doing Airbnb round here," that's your cue to look elsewhere.

### Step 5: Run the Numbers Through the Deal Analyser

Once you've got your data, plug it into a proper deal analysis. Here's what you need to calculate:

- **Monthly revenue** (nightly rate x occupancy x 30)
- **Monthly costs** (rent, council tax, utilities, Wi-Fi, cleaning, linen, insurance, Airbnb fees, maintenance fund)
- **Monthly profit** (revenue minus costs)
- **Setup costs** (furnishing, photography, safety compliance, licensing)
- **Months to breakeven** (setup costs divided by monthly profit)

If the monthly profit is below £500 or the breakeven is longer than 4 months, the deal probably isn't strong enough. Move on and find a better one.
